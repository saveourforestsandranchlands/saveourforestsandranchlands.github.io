Rangelands

Rangelands are defined as lands that are unsuitable for crop or timber production. Worldwide, they are generally too dry, too high, too unpredictable, too steep, or with soils too shallow or rocky for other agricultural uses. Much of central San Diego county fits this definition, and the original European settlers were those who knew how to use the land for extensive livestock production. Land grants in the nineteenth century acknowledged the extensive nature of the industry, and usually consisted of more than a thousand acres. While there is no absolute definition of how many livestock are required to support a family ranching operation, it is generally estimated at about 200-300 brood cows for a cow-calf operation of the type typical of California (Holechek et al. 1995). Most often, income from livestock is part rather than all of household income. Concurrent crop production, off-farm income, and hunting leases are among the sources of income that have been identified as making up the rest (Huntsinger et al.1997, Bartlett et al. 1989, Richards and George 1996, Johnson 1998, Hargreave 1993). Ranching, however, is considered an important part of personal and family identity for those with range livestock (Richards and George 1996).

The forage production of the rangelands of central San Diego County is based on the annual grasses that flourish in the Mediterranean, wet winter climate. They grow rapidly when water is available and temperatures are warm in the late winter and early Spring, setting abundant seed in the late Spring that germinates with sufficient fall rain. Extensive livestock production must cope with highly variable, rainfall dependent annual production, and long, dry summers. Palatable shrubs, moist meadows and swales, upland pastures with cooler climates, desert pastures, and the feeding of hay, agricultural byproducts, supplements and other forages helps to smooth the unpredictability in climate, and to carry herds through the dry periods. Herds can be moved in and out of the county to take advantage of pastures in other areas at certain times of the year; stocker grazing operations, where young cattle are purchased every year to take advantage of the winter and spring feed, and then sold when the forage is gone, are also a common strategy for utilizing this type of rangeland.

Rangeland grazing generally requires relatively large acreages that provide forage and habitat diversity, as the palatability and quality of different species and vegetation types varies at different times of year. Range livestock, unlike closely confined domestic animals and crops, can move themselves to obtain water and therefore discrete, fairly widely dispersed water sources are adequate for extensive range grazing. While of relatively low value on a per acre basis, extensive production also requires relatively low investment per acre, and of the various crops that can be produced on San Diego’s rangelands, requires the least disturbance of biota and soils. Of course, an individual producer may make the decision to utilize more aggressive techniques. Typical modifications that may be part of a range livestock production program include development of reliable water sources, interseeding with legumes and pasture grasses, and removal or thinning of brush or dense oaks. In recent decades, removal of oaks by livestock producers (once encouraged and even subsidized by the state) has declined significantly in California (Huntsinger et al. 1997) as ranchers have become aware of the value of oaks to forage production (Frost and McDougald 1989), and for wildlife and amenity values.

That is not to say that rangeland grazing does not have impacts on the biota and soils. Like any agricultural endeavor, grazing can cause changes in species composition, soil stability, and water quality. However, these impacts can be controlled and minimized with appropriate management practices.

Central San Diego County Rangelands

The (20) designated rangelands we examined are of productivities typical of southern California rangelands (McDougald et al. 1991). These lands have been used for rangeland grazing for more than a hundred years (Burcham 1981[1957]). Livestock production and production practices have shaped the present Central San Diego landscape of chaparral slopes, and grassy oak and grassland valleys with wide open vistas and a very natural appearance. Generations of San Diegans have enjoyed this landscape, often without even being aware of its agricultural function. Wildlife and watershed values have to a great extent been retained. Extensive livestock production requires relatively large acreages, but makes use of a wide variety of lands, including open chaparral, woodlands, scrublands, meadows and upland grasslands. This variety is also part of extensive livestock production, where the goal is to carry livestock through the year (or part of the year in the case of some systems) using as little expensive feed as possible. Vegetation types and localities of different climate complement each other. Ideally, the livestock producer has access to a mix of climatic and vegetation types that provide feed during different times of the year. Sometimes, part of that menu is public rangeland. Sometimes, it is land leased from other people.

The herbaceous component of Central San Diegan rangelands is mostly annual grasses, with varying proportions of native needlegrass and other perennial grasses. Much of the range is chaparral intermingled with open grassland and woodland grass. At higher elevations, woodland grass and chaparral are intermingled with broadleaf and coniferous trees. Grading into the desert to the east are semidesert shrubs, annual grasses and broadleaf herbs, and perennial grasses such as desert needlegrass. Moist meadows and swales, though small, are highly productive often even through the summer, and are an important complement to the surrounding dry range in an extensive grazing system. USDA Forest Service analysis of some of the meadows in the Area (20) designated lands reveals exceptionally high productivities four to five times that of upland range.

The NRCS (cite) identifies four major climatic zones for rangeland in San Diego county, all of which are found in the areas under consideration: 1. the Southern California Coastal Plains (Pala-Pauma and Ramona), 2. the western part of the Southern California mountains (North Mountain and Julian), 3. The eastern part of the Southern California mountains (minimally present), and 4. the Sonoran basin and range (Desert SRA). Cooler air and fog make the Southern California Coastal Plains climate more moderate, higher elevations result in cooler temperatures and higher rainfall in the western Southern California Mountains, and the Sonoran Basin and Range is characterized by very low rainfall and very high summer temperatures. These factors affect the quality and temporal distribution of forage production at each site. We will discuss the rangeland characteristics of larger (20) designated areas in five planning areas: Pala Pauma, Ramona, Julian, North Mountain, and Desert SRA. It should be noted that this is not an all-inclusive analysis. Mapped soil types most common in each study area are examined. More precise information could be obtained through the use of GIS data layers and technology available to the county.

Pala-Pauma Planning Area

Vegetation types within the Pala Pauma (20) designated areas of more than 1000 acres that are typically used for grazing include Englemann and coast live oak woodlands, annual and perennial grasslands, mixed chaparral, and coastal sage scrub. Statewide, extensive livestock grazing is the most common agricultural use of oak woodland, grassland, and chaparral or scrub lands (Ewing et al. 1988).

The predominant soils in the Pala-Pauma (20) areas fall into two "Range Sites" described by the NRCS and termed "Loamy Range Site 19" and "Shallow Loamy Range Site 19" by the NRCS (SCS and USDA Forest Service, 1973), with the former the most common. Range Sites are defined for a geographical area by a combination of soil, slope, and climatic characteristics that produce specific vegetation and forage types. Range sites do not include land considered unsuitable for grazing by the Natural Resource Conservation Service: for example, areas with such abundant rocky outcrops that they prevent animal access or produce no forage. These two Range Sites do not include all the lands in these (20) areas: small pockets of differing soils are no doubt present. Narrow or small riparian areas are present and augment productivity, but are not reflected in these production figures. This is, rather, the dryland range typical of the area.

Annual forage produced on rangelands for livestock grazing is generally presented in pounds per acre. Monitoring of productivity over years provides typical forage production for various range sites in low rainfall and high rainfall years. It is general practice in California to plan for somewhere in between. The pounds per acre produced on a range site is then modified by the amount that can be grazed by livestock without damaging soils or plant productivity. This results in "Available" forage, which is forage available for grazing on a sustainable basis. Finally, this is converted to "AUMs", Animal-Unit-Months. AUMs are the amount of forage needed to support one breeding cow for one month. Horses require a bit more than one AUM, sheep .20 AUM.

As has been earlier described, AUMs on private rangeland are typically supplemented by feed, public range, wet meadows or pasture, agricultural by products, supplements and so forth. For example, Forest Service grazing leases are often for 2-3 months of the year in the summer. During the rest of the year, the herd is carried on private ground, other leased pasture, feed and so forth. Two hundred leased AUMs on Forest Service land may typically be supporting one hundred head for part of the year. Sometimes, this is a part of the year when other options are limited or expensive.

Available forage productivity can be affected also by increases in unpalatable species, or dense trees and shrubs. Low densities of trees—for example the open oak savanna found in central San Diego County – may result in greater than expected forage production or better temporal distribution of forage quality (Frost and McDougald 1989). Some shrubs are also grazed by livestock and can be used, particularly during times of the year when the grass is dry. General descriptions of the two major range types in this area follow (SCS and USDA 1973):

Loamy Range 19: The topography of this type of site ranges from gently sloping to very steep. Soils are coarse sandy loams, sandy loams, very fine sandy loams, and loams that are moderately deep to very deep over bedrock. All are moderately to somewhat excessively well drained. Cover is generally grass and shrubs or grass. About half the cover may be ceanothus, California sagebrush, sumac, chamise, flattop buckwheat, black sage and other shrubs, and the rest is soft chess, wild oats, filaree, needlegrass, and other annual grasses and plants. In many areas, removal of brush can increase forage yields by two to four times (SCS and USDA, 1973).
Shallow Loamy Range 19: The topography of this site is moderately sloping to very steep. Soils are eroded coarse sandy loams to loams that are shallow and very shallow over igneous rock or sandstone. They are rocky or stony in many places, and well drained to somewhat excessively drained. The site has brush cover, mostly of chamise or flattop buckwheat and California sagebrush along with small amounts of sumac, ceanothus, and other species of chaparral. The herbaceous plant cover is a mixture of soft chess, wild oaks, filaree, and other annual and perennial grasses and plants.

Our examination of the more common soil types found in this area indicate that most of them fall into NRCS "capability groupings" that indicate severe limitations for agricultural use. In almost every case, NRCS recommends limiting their use to pasture, range, woodland, or wildlife habitat.

Ancillary Benefits

Although the primary purpose of the (20) land use designation is to facilitate extensive agricultural use of the land, the protection of extensive agriculture, especially rangeland grazing, can also produce substantial additional public benefits. Grassland and oak woodland, the vegetation types whose highest and best sustainable use is livestock grazing, also provide some of the state’s best wildlife habitat, protect watersheds, and furnish visual amenities. In contrast, intensive agriculture of the sort envisioned for (20) designated land in General Plan Amendment GPA 96-03 does not provide these ancillary public benefits at levels anywhere near those of grazing land. Intensive agriculture also results in much greater soil and vegetation disturbance than rangeland grazing.

The California Wildlife-Habitat Relationship (WHR) System, maintained by the California Department of Fish and Game, is the state’s most comprehensive wildlife-habitat analysis tool. Using the WHR classification of habitat types, the WHR system produces lists of wildlife species associated with particular vegetation types (Meyer and Laudenslayer 1988). San Diego County’s (20) designated land commonly used for livestock grazing includes WHR habitat types of coastal oak woodland, montane hardwood-conifer, montane hardwood, montane riparian, valley foothill riparian, and annual grassland. The database also includes species lists for habitat types likely to be found if agriculture is intensified in the County’s (20) designated lands: cropland, orchard-vineyard, and urban. The intensified agricultural production categories greatly reduce biodiversity.

The number of wildlife species, especially native species, is considerably larger in habitat types used for livestock grazing than in the more intensive cultivated habitat types. Indeed, California’s oak woodlands, where the predominant use is livestock grazing, are home to a greater diversity of wildlife species than any other major habitat type in the state (Standiford 1999; Mayer et al. 1986). In contrast, according to the California Wildlife Habitat Relationships Database habitat descriptions for "cropland" and "orchard-vineyard" types, "Croplands have greatly reduced the wildlife richness and diversity of California," and "Larger and more diverse populations of wildlife were supported by the native habitats [displaced by orchards-vineyards]" (Meyer and Laudenslayer 1988, pp. 138, 140).

In addition to the wildlife value of the grazing land habitat types per se, the large and contiguous nature of grazing lands is an important factor in their biological value. When habitat is reduced in size and isolated by intervening cropland or housing, the survival of some wildlife and plant populations can be jeopardized (Saunders et al. 1991; Soulé et al. 1988). Cropland, orchards, and rural residential development of the type proposed for the (20) designated lands are likely to fragment habitat to a much greater degree than extensive rangeland grazing operations. For example, a recent study in Sonoma County’s hardwood rangeland found that the percent of neotropical bird species was significantly higher in large (> 300 acres) parcels than in 10 - 40 acre ranchettes or 0.5 –2.5 acre single-family home lots (Merenlender and Heise 1999).

Watershed values / Soil and vegetation disturbance

Another notable public benefit produced by rangelands is watershed protection. Most of California’s water supply flows through hardwood rangelands (Standiford 1999). Water quality would likely deteriorate if grazing land were replaced by intensive agriculture. The impact of livestock grazing on water quality is small compared to that of intensive agricultural activities. Fertilizer and pesticide use on rangelands is minimal. In contrast, intensive agriculture typically employs petro-chemical inputs. Numerous, intensive agricultural operations spread over a large area of the County’s (20) designated lands may cause significant non-point source water pollution problems that prove difficult to solve. Soil disturbance and changes in vegetation type and vegetation cover are commonly limited in rangeland livestock operations. Clearing of vegetation and disturbance of soil is likely to occur at much higher levels and with greater frequency as intensive agricultural activities increase.

Visual amenities

Large, contiguous areas of rangeland provide open space viewsheds available to the public, even when the land is privately owned (Huntsinger and Hopkinson 1996). Under the scenario envisioned in the General Plan Amendment, grazing land would be interspersed with numerous small farms and houses. The open space character of the (20) designated lands would likely be changed significantly. In addition, the interspersing of houses and ranchlands increases costs to livestock producers.

Neighbor issues

In a region under intense development pressure, such as San Diego County, small minimum lot size zoning will result in the rapid advance of housing into agricultural zones, especially those in which the per acre agricultural return is fairly low.

Research shows that as residential development disperses into rangeland areas, the business and cultural practices of livestock ranching are hindered (Hargrave 1993; Forero et al. 1991). Increased liability costs, trespass, vandalism, careless use of gates, and marauding pet dogs hamper the smooth operation of ranching, reducing not only the financial returns of the business but also satisfaction with the lifestyle that ranchers say is such a vital part of their compensation (Liffmann and Huntsinger 2000; Smith and Martin 1972). Restrictions on traditional management practices, such as prescribed burning, may be imposed on ranching operations, and informal customs of dispute resolution may break down as ex-urbanites move into agricultural areas (Ellickson 1991, 1986).

In addition, as the demand for houses rises in agricultural areas, land prices increase, and real estate speculation becomes more frequent. All these factors foster what researchers have termed the "impermanence syndrome" (Heimlich and Anderson 1987). Spurred by inflated land values and demoralized by conflicts with urban neighbors, ranchers begin to view their livestock operation as a "holding pattern" preceding residential development. Land stewardship may taper off as resource sustainability loses its importance as a long-term goal (Heimlich and Anderson 1987).

As residential development increases and ranchers leave the business, a threshold may be reached past which ranching is no longer viable in the region (Liffmann and Huntsinger 2000). A critical mass of livestock operations is needed in any ranching region to support the industry’s infrastructure and to provide informal labor pools (Hart 1991).

Facilitating livestock operations in the (20) designated lands will necessitate, among other strategies mentioned below, discouraging widespread residential development. Minimum lot sizes greater than those put forward in the General Plan Amendment are required to achieve this aim. William W. Wood, an agricultural economist, asserts that due to conflicts likely to occur when ex-urbanites move into agricultural areas, "almost a zero density [of residences] is about the only compatible one" (quoted in Hart 1991, p. 37). In the San Francisco Bay Area, a region that also faces intense development pressure on its grazing lands, minimum lot sizes are set at 60 acres in three counties, 100 acres in one county, and 160 acres in three counties (Schuyler et al. 1996).

Ranching viability

Researchers have recognized for some time that estate taxes present a major problem for ranchers who typically have small cash reserves but substantial assets in land (Johnson 1998). However, selling off small parcels of ranch land to pay taxes is not generally viewed as a solution that results in the long-term preservation of ranching operations or rangelands. Rather, it is a slow "death by a thousand cuts," especially when residential development occurs on the parcels sold. Other techniques for addressing the problem of estate taxes, such as conservation easements, show greater promise (Wright 1994; Wright 1993). Conservation easements and economic incentives for land conservation can also serve as sources of operating capital.

Preserving livestock operations and the extensive land base they require is more than just a matter of minimum lot size zoning. However, as experience in other California counties has shown, zoning for large minimum lot sizes is an important first step in a comprehensive planning strategy (Huntsinger and Hopkinson 1996; Hart 1991). In Marin County, such zoning reduced residential development pressure while ranchers, county planners, and others developed a lasting solution for the preservation of ranching. Had large minimum lot sizes not been imposed, few ranches would have survived to be preserved (Hart 1991).

In counties contending with enormous development pressure, such as Marin County and Contra Costa County, minimum lot zoning has provided only temporary, albeit vital, protection. The agricultural land base continues to dwindle because real estate buyers are willing to purchase large lots for non-agricultural purposes. Zoning is also vulnerable to changes in the political climate (Huntsinger and Hopkinson 1996).

Long-term solutions that are successfully protecting livestock grazing and its land base in other California counties and in areas across the Western U. S. include:

• firm urban limit boundaries,

• conservation easements / land trust programs,

• coalitions between ranchers, conservationists, and government planners, and

• incentive programs for land owners (Huntsinger and Hopkinson 1996).

Summary/Conclusions

1. Rangeland grazing is an extensive land use that, compared to field agriculture, requires minimal disruption of wildlife andwildlife habitat, soils, and watershed. Most of the (20) designated areas have been used for livestock grazing for a hundred years or more, and the present landscape has been shaped by that land use.

2. The rangelands of the (20) designated lands are productive southern California rangelands. Upland range is complemented by meadowlands, public range, and other sources of feed. Typical Southern Californian grasslands have expected productivities of .1 to .7 Animal Unit Months depending on slope and with a tree or shrub canopy cover of less than 50% (McDougald et al. 1991). The upland range soils of the (20) designated areas are quite productive by this standard. Forest Service analysis of mountain meadow types in the area show that they exceed this productivity by several times.

3. The Soil Survey of San Diego County puts the common soils of the (20) designated lands into "capability groupings" that indicate severe limitations for agricultural use. Almost exclusively, NRCS recommends limiting their use to pasture, range, woodland, or wildlife habitat.

4. Intermingling of residential development and ranch land results in conflicts that increase costs and difficulties to producers. As the number of producers declines, so does the supporting industrial infrastructure, as well as informal support networks. Residential development often tends to occur first in areas of vital importance to livestock producers because of their high productivity: meadowlands and oak woodlands. Loss of these lands to development renders upland range less usable.

5. Conservation of grazing lands and ranches is a goal of a variety of programs in the western United States. It is not easy, but potentially can provide a wide range of public goods, as well as agricultural products and the conservation of a historically significant way of life.

[1] Annual forage produced on rangelands for livestock grazing is generally presented in pounds per acre. Monitoring of productivity over years provides typical forage production for various range sites in low rainfall and high rainfall years. It is general practice in California to plan for somewhere in between. The pounds per acre produced on a range site is then modified by the amount that can be grazed by livestock without damaging soils or plant productivity. This results in "Available" forage, which is forage available for grazing on a sustainable basis. Finally, this is converted to "AUMs", Animal-Unit-Months. AUMs are the amount of forage needed to support one breeding cow for one month. Horses require a bit more than one AUM, sheep .20 AUM.

As has been earlier described, AUMs on private rangeland are typically supplemented by feed, public range, wet meadows or pasture, agricultural by products, supplements and so forth. For example, Forest Service grazing leases are often for 2-3 months of the year in the summer. During the rest of the year, the herd is carried on private ground, other leased pasture, feed and so forth. Two hundred leased AUMs on Forest Service land may typically be supporting one hundred head for part of the year. Sometimes, this is a part of the year when other options are limited or expensive.

Available forage productivity can be affected also by increases in unpalatable species, or dense trees and shrubs. Low densities of trees—for example the open oak savannahs found in central San Diego County – may result in greater than expected forage production or better temporal distribution of forage quality (Frost and McDougald, 1989). Some shrubs are also grazed by livestock and can be used, particularly during times of the year when the grass is dry.
