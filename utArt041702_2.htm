We've lost track of how many times the Board of Supervisors has been shot down in court over its faulty plan to protect agricultural land in San Diego County's back-country. And who knows how many millions of dollars in taxpayers' money was wasted in the supervisors' legal contortions to avoid showing a true picture of how much residential development and environmental damage their proposed agricultural plan would cause?

But they're at it again. Today, the Board of Supervisors will consider yet another revision to the environmental study of its land- use plan for 250,000 acres of private agricultural land. And, again, the flaws are glaring.

The latest environmental document concludes that a significant increase in demand for back-country residential development is unlikely, an astonishing claim in a county where housing demand is rocketing upward. What's more, the methodology used to gauge the environmental impact of the plan has been labeled "simply bad" by local biology professors.

For decades, the county has been doing back-flips trying to justify a land-use plan that claims to preserve agriculture acreage while in reality paving the way for development. But every time planning officials are challenged in court, the nonprofit group Save Our Forests and Ranchlands (SOFAR) shoots holes in their environmental studies, and a judge rules against the county.

In 1996, Judge Judith McConnell found that the county's stewardship of agricultural lands was so inept that she declared a building moratorium and awarded land-use control of the 250,000 acres to SOFAR. Today, if you live on that land and want to do a room addition, you apply to SOFAR for a permit, not the county.

In the past six years, the county has lost three major court decisions and many smaller judgments as well. San Diego County residents should be furious that their elected leaders have lost their land-use authority for six years because of their own dereliction. But that's what has happened. And this latest attempt probably won't change things.

"There is not likely to be a significant increase in the demand for residential development within (agricultural) lands once the moratorium is lifted and the Plan Amendment is implemented," the county's latest environmental document reads. The implication is that nobody needs to worry about increased development because there's little demand for development. What utter nonsense.

Meanwhile, the methodology for gauging environmental impacts consists of developing a theoretical, one-size-fits-all footprint for what a typical residential home and lot might be, and dividing biological resources such as endangered plants evenly among each theoretical lot. Then, county researchers studied those theoretical lots to determine the impact of the agricultural plan. Biology professors Ted Case of UCSD and Jay Diffendorfer of SDSU called that approach "so at odds with what we feel well-trained biologists would perform that we can only conclude political or other influences warped their methods."

The supervisors should abandon this agricultural plan, which clearly cannot pass scientific muster. If supervisors want large- scale residential development in the back-country, they should say so. But if they really want to protect agriculture and natural resources, they need to adopt planning and zoning rules that will actually do it.
