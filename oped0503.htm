June 2003, Opinion/Editorial

San Diego Union-Tribune, Thursday, June 5, 2003
East County Opinion
GP2020: DOES THE COUNTY REALLY HAVE A PLAN?

Five years ago, the San Diego County Board of Supervisors embarked on a multi-million dollar effort to revise the San Diego County General Plan, the land use "constitution" that sets the zoning pattern for the vast unincorporated areas of the County. The stated purpose of this planning effort, called General Plan 2020 (GP2020), was to be a "comprehensive update" of the County Plan for land development, roads, water supplies and other infrastructure until, as the name would suggest, the year 2020.

Of course once a plan is established, it must be implemented and there must be a commitment by the County to adhere to it. Otherwise all the dollars and time spent on the plan will be wasted. That is why a recent action by the Board of Supervisors brings into question its dedication to the planning effort.

Previously the County's General Plan could be changed by a developer only after satisfying threshold criteria set forth in Board of Supervisor Policy I-63. That Policy gave equal standing to a Community Planning Group and the Director of Planning and Land Use and required 6 out of 10 findings to be made before a change to a Community's General Plan could be initiated. Negative determinations, by either the affected Planning Group or the Director, sent the application to a public hearing before the Board of Supervisors for a final decision. So why did the Board of Supervisors rewrite a Policy that has been in effect for almost 30 years and was overwhelmingly supported by Community Planning and Sponsor Groups?

It is now known that there was a concerted effort by the development community to eliminate Policy I-63. A memo from Stonegate Development, submitted at the April 30th hearing indicates a long-standing effort to eliminate or at least seriously revise the Policy's requirements. A memo from this company, read from at the hearing stated "Success on our part on the Policy I-63, Section III exemption could open the floodgate for projects county-wide."

By eliminating the Policy I-63 findings, the Board of Supervisors has more than fulfilled the wishes of the development industry; it has opened the floodgates for speculative development in the County and made a mockery of the very concept of the General Plan.

So what are the threshold requirements for changing the County's General Plan under the new rewritten Policy?

"It is policy of the Board of Supervisors that any amendment to the County General Plan must be initiated by the Planning Director, Planning Commission, or Board of Supervisors. All requests must be filed initially with and reviewed by the Planning Director. The request shall be accompanied by the appropriate fees paid
pursuant to Section 362 of the San Diego County Administrative Code."

There are none! There are no findings and no requirement for a county hearing if the elected Planning Group opposes proceeding with a General Plan Amendment (GPA). In fact, it will not be possible for a community planning or sponsor group to appeal a developer's proposed change to their community plan, if it is approved by the Planning Director. On the other hand, if the Planning Director denies a developer's request to process a GPA, he can appeal to the Planning Commission. At the Commission, an approval cannot be appealed. However, a denial of the developer's general plan may be appealed to the Board of Supervisors. Hard to believe, but true!

Actually, it is not even clear if the County can legally deny a request since there are no findings. So what does this mean for GP 2020?

Extending the quote form the Stonegate Memorandum, "Success on our part on the Policy I-63, Section III exemption could open the floodgate for projects county-wide. I expect even the Supervisors will try to avoid this."

The Development Community succeeded, beyond their wildest dreams. With the general plan amendment threshold process fully politicized, the "floodgates" are open and GP2020 and County General Plan is a farce.

What can be done?

When representative government breaks down, the people can become the powers that be, through the Ballot Initiative Process. The time has come.

Gordon Shackelford is a member, and past Chair of the Lakeside Community Planning Group
