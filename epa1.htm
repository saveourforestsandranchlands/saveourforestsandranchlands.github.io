Dear Supervisor Jacob:

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has recently received a copy of an EIR for "Agricultural Issues General Plan Amendment and Implementing Rezone (GPA 96-03)." We understand that though the EIR comment period has past, this rezone issue will be before the San Diego County Board of Supervisors on April 5, 2000. The purpose of this letter is to alert you to a number of concerns we have regarding significant land use changes, such as would be enabled by adoption of GPA 96-03, and hope that you and the other Board members will consider EPA's views in your deliberations.

The County proposes to redesignate over 200,000 acres of Agricultural Preserve (Ag 20) lands to establish a minimum parcel size of 10 acres per dwelling unit for Ag 20 lands west of the County Water Authority boundary and 40 acres per dwelling unit for Ag 20 lands east of the County Water Authority boundary. According to information provided in the EIR (pages 2-4 to 2-15), the proposed project will significantly increase the density of dwellings on both sides of the County Water Authority boundary. For instance, the overall existing density for Ag 20 lands is 137.5 acres per dwelling unit (ac/du), with much lower densities for specific planning areas (e.g., 1,359 ac/du for the Desert Subregional Planning Area; 534 ac/du for the Mountain Empire Subregional Planning Area; 430 ac/du for the Julian Community Planning Area; and 335ac/du for the Pala/Pauma Valley Subregional Planning Area). EPA is concerned that the proposed project will contribute to the degradation of several watersheds in San Diego County. Upzoning agricultural land may be the first step towards more intense agricultural practices, with concomitant increases in land clearing and the use of water and hazardous chemicals, and subsequent subdivision into housing and commercial development, with associated roads and other infrastructure. The environmental consequences of these actions can include the loss and/or fragmentation of terrestrial and aquatic habitats, decrease in the region's biodiversity, and degradation of water quality from increased pollutant loadings.

A particularly troubling aspect of the proposed amendment is that, notwithstanding the fact that GPA 96-03 is expected to result in significant adverse impacts to the environment, including water and biological resources, no feasible mitigation measures have been identified (EIR, ES-3).

That GPA 96-03 will lead to intensified agricultural and residential/commercial development is indisputable. EPA cautions the county not to underestimate both the amount and significance of water quality impacts that will result. Already, there are 36 federally-listed impaired water bodies in San Diego County (per 1998 Clean Water Act Section 303d List). An increase in the number of impaired water bodies will most likely occur with the proposed conversion of over 200,000 acres of existing grazing land and open space.

During the last 150 years, most of coastal San Diego County habitats have been lost to

agriculture and development. California has lost more than 90 percent of its wetlands (as well as most its coastal sage scrub habitat) - - the highest rate of loss in the country. These losses will continue through both regulated and unregulated activities. These activities include converting rangelands to intensive agriculture, draining of wetlands, damaging aquatic sites with off-road vehicles, and developing low-­density "ranchette" housing. This type of development can subtly destroy sensitive areas by damming, draining, and diverting wetlands; and by introducing year-round livestock grazing. These practices retard the ability of wetlands to absorb and filter precipitation and runoff, and result in increased discharges of pollutants to surface- and ground waters such as sediment, fertilizers, herbicides, pesticides, detergents, and oil. These pollution burdens are difficult to solve by using non-point source tools alone.

GPA 96-03 represents immediate threats to wetlands and surface-and ground waters through:

(1) the subdivision of existing parcels into smaller "ranchette" parcels;

(2) changing livestock species, grazing patterns, and stocking rates -- from relatively dispersed cattle grazing to intensive grazing by horses;

(3) conversion of rangeland into row crops, nurseries, and orchards;

(4) loss of percolation areas and increased pumping of groundwater,

(5) the conversion of unirrigated rangeland to irrigated recreational lands (e.g., golf courses and the introduction of fertilizers, herbicides, and pesticides; and,

(6) subdivision of the existing parcels into conventional suburban development.

An additional concern is that the anticipated development of Ag 20 lands that are tributary to surface water reservoirs will impact the quality and quantity of the region's drinking water supply.

Examples of drinking water reservoirs at potential risk are the Otay, Sutherland, Hodges, and El Capitan reservoirs.
