March 2000, Opinion/Editorial

This is "Smart Growth"?

County's New Plan will make matters much worse.
By Duncan McFetridge

Beneath the gloss of good times, there's a growing sense of crisis in San Diego County. Daily experience validates it: Freeways are growing more crowded and the open spaces we value are being destroyed by leap-frogging subdivisions. If San Diego does not check its pattern of far-flung suburban development, our quality of rural and urban life will continue to slide downhill with expensive and irreversible results. And there's a sense of frustration. City and county residents fear that our elected officials, having failed to address these problems for years, are doing little to solve them now.

The most recent case of what we will politely call planning failure comes before the Board of Supervisors on April 5, in the form of GPA 96-03, an amendment to county's General Plan. It sounds like another boring slab of bureaucracy, but it could have far reaching effects on water quality, traffic, air, beaches and public services throughout the county.

Back in 1996 the Superior Court, responding to a lawsuit brought by Save Our Forests and Ranchlands (SOFAR), declared the county's General Plan illegal. The General Plan is supposed to regulate land use in the public interest, not just by preventing sprawl; it must also preserve critical natural and economic resources. Because it lacked mandatory preservation policies and placed development zoning on our wild and agricultural lands, the Plan violated several important state laws. The county was ordered to come back in six months with a legally sound preservation plan. Now, after four years of fiddling while Rome burns, they are back with this proposed change.

A look at the amendment shows that while it purports to "facilitate agriculture," in reality it gives developers -- and those developers in overalls, the Farm Bureau --license to strip our back country clean.

The county's proposal offers none of the protections they were ordered to come up with. In the name of agriculture, the proposal would actually allow the clearing, grading and subdivision of 200,000 acres, an area twice the size of Camp Pendleton, into ranchettes. This is "smart growth" ? Even worse, the county's own Environmental Impact Report admits that the plan will have county-wide, unmitigable impacts to traffic, air, water, biology, geology, archaeology and scenery. Just to be clear, "unmitigable" doesn't mean "unfortunate": it means devastating and irreversible.

A valid land use plan should help conserve, not waste, water for the entire county. It should encourage sustainable agriculture and discourage high-irrigation, chemically-managed farming which is completely inappropriate to our back country. It should preserve rich grazing and open ranch lands, and forests that present and future generations will need. It should buffer our precious national forests from condo complexes and freeway off-ramps.

Because the proposal fails to distinguish between appropriate and inappropriate agricultural uses for the back country, and because it has no enforcement mechanisms, it is grossly inadequate.

SOFAR has an alternative. We propose large lot zoning, with mandatory environmental review and enforcement for any clearing and subdivision of land. Our alternative is supported by the Audubon Society, the Sierra Club, the California Native Plant Society; local civil groups including the Vulcan Mountain Association, the Ramona Crest and Boulevard Sponsor groups, and eminent biologists, including UCSD's Ted Case, SDSU's Ellen Bauder and Michael Soule of the national Wildlands Project.

It's a travesty to label lands "resource conservation areas" and "highest environmental value," and then fail to protect them. It's a scandal that once again the same politicians who have resisted taking real measures to protect our county's quality of life are offering such a deeply flawed plan. At a time when public concern over land use is boiling over, our elected leaders talk about "smart growth” while cooking up another scheme to ease the way for inappropriate development. We have almost no time left to do things right: as the recent week-long sewage leak showed, San Diego County's infrastructure is decades behind it's pattern of rapid suburban growth. Adequate public transportation is still a daydream, and many of our urban areas are staggering under long years of disinvestment and neglect.

Recent history shows that sprawl and the destruction of the back country have not been slowed by the Board of Supervisors and their planning department. Far from it. Only grassroots activism, including ballot initiatives and citizen-led lawsuits, has been effective. Once again, it's up to the public to protect San Diego county from phony planning.

We must insist that the Board of Supervisors reject GPA 96-03.

Duncan McFetridge, President
Save Our Forests and Ranchlands
