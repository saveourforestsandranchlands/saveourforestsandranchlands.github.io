Subject: EIR for San Diego County GPA 96-03

Dear Nancy Woo,

I received a copy of your April 4th letter in which you substantially alter the statements you made in a March 31st letter about water quality impacts associated with the County's GPA 96-03. I am writing to share with you the results of your revised letter at the April 5th Board hearing on GPA 96-03.

In your first letter, you correctly perceived and concluded that the massive chance in type of land use and increase in density over 200,000 acres of San Diego's wildlands would have enormous water quality impacts and put drinking water reservoirs at risk. This letter rightfully caused great concern on the part of public officials and agencies in charge of watershed management. Your first letter was absolutely true and based on a correct description of both the project and the nature of the lands in question.

The on-ground density of the lands evaluated in the EIR for GPA 96-03, east of the County Water Authority tine, is roughly one dwelling unit per 600 acres as revealed in the EIR itself. In many cases, huge grazing areas like the Guejuita Ranch, Santa Ysabel Valley, and San Felipe Ranch have a density of one home per 5,000 acres. In the County’s proposal, they evaluated the effects of "a total clearing" of the landscape in these areas and a change from on-ground density to a planning density of one dwelling unit per forty acres. This change from ground to plan represents roughly a 500% increase in population density with all associated impacts.

On the question of land use, the assumption of "total clearing" was based on the concept of a radical change in use from undisturbed wildland agriculture to intensive agriculture that would radically alter the landscape.

The EIR amazingly stated that once overriding considerations were stoned for the unmitigable impacts in virtually every CEQA category, that there would be no further environmental review for clearing of lands for agricultural uses.

In one of the most naked displays of political manipulation, the County seized upon a phrase in your first letter that characterized the change in density as an "UPZONE." They apparently tricked the EPA into believing that this was wrong and that since "the current zoning was 8-acres, the 40-acre proposal was instead a DOWNZONE" and a decrease in density that would have beneficial impacts!

This is political chicanery at its worst.

It is especially egregious because County officials took advantage of your lack of knowledge of technical terminology knowing full well that to compare the current proposal with what the Courts ruled to be totally illegal (8-acre zoning) was what CEQA calls a "Plan to Plan Comparison" which is expressly PROHIBITED BY LAW in evaluating the impacts of a proposed project.

Specifically, courts have held that where a proposed project is an agency decision changing planning or zoning designations, its potential impacts should not be compared only with those that would ultimately occur, with buildout, under an existing plan. Rather, the analysis should focus on "existing physical conditions" before comparing the eventual future conditions that would result under build out pursuant to both the existing and contemplated plan. CEQA Guidelines Section 15125 (e), Christward Ministry v. Superior Court (4th Dist. 1986) 184 Cal.App. 3d 180, 190-191 (quoted from the guide to the California Environmental Quality Act authored by Michael H. Remy and Tina A. Thomas, 1999, page 375.)

Eight acre zoning on Ag Preserve land was invalidated by Court action. The only comparison allowed by CEQA is an evaluation of the project's impacts against the actual environment as it exists on the ground today. Thus, I am affirming again that your original letter was right in both form and content. The 40-acre density represents a MASSIVE INCREASE compared to the ground.

Comparing the forty acres to the former illegal eight acres was wrong, deceitful, and ultimately meaningless. The EPA was deceived into changing their first letter in order to serve special political interests prior to one of the most important land use decisions in San Diego's history. As a result of your second letter, the Supervisors and planning staff apparently felt that one of the most significant unmitigable impacts of the project -water quality­ was no longer an item for discussion.

They passed GPA 96-03 unanimously. They failed to note that the EPA continued to correctly acknowledge the impacts of the project on regional water quality.

We deeply regret that the EPA was subject to the kind of political intrigue that compromised your integrity and that must have embarrassed your staff. Rest assured, Save Our Forests will pursue this matter and vindicate your original judgment as to the actual impacts of the County's proposal.

Sincerely yours,
