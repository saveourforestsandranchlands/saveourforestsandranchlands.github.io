General Plan Amendment 2020


Talking ‘Smart Growth,’ doing nothing

By Duncan McFetridge

Imagine living in a county in which elected officials are so controlled by developer interests that a judge removes the county's authority over hundreds of thousands of acres of rural lands and orders the county to clean up its act.

"Submit a new plan that meets state law," this courageous judge declares, "and you county supervisors and planners can reclaim your privilege to administer these lands."

Then imagine that rather than rushing to meet the judge's six-month deadline, the Board of Supervisors and their lawyers spend more than $2 million over five years to fight the court by developing three consecutive "new" plans, each one of which, worse than the other, is bounced into the planning trash can by the judge.

With the final plan negatively impacting 200,000 acres of watershed, threatening the region's drinking water supply, and bringing in the state attorney general against the county, the judge finds that the county utterly fails to meet state laws that would protect the most biologically diverse wildlands contained in a single county within the continental United States.

There's more.

After the judge's most recent ruling, the county supervisors again choose courtroom wrangling over compliance with state law. They are taking their appeal to the state's higher court s, hoping they can rehabilitate policies that will spread suburbs and malls into the backcountry.

Incredible, right? It's no fantasy. By now, you've guessed that I'm talking about San Diego County, from 1996 right up to today.

What makes this a quintessential San Diegan dream-like, inside-out, upside-down story is that instead of responding with solutions to the public's growing alarm at traffic gridlock, beach pollution, housing costs, decaying infrastructure and special interests' control of government, the supervisors squander county money and employee hours on "smart growth" seminars and symposia held monthly around the county.

What is this "smart growth" that is staged San Diego-style and supported by the same elected officials who repeatedly go to the mat for big developers? We've heard a lot about smart growth recently, but are given few clues to help us tell the difference between sound planning and empty rhetoric.

We should start by making a distinction between what the county says and what the county does. In theory, smart growth emphasizes protecting watersheds, wildlands and ranches by concentrating new and redeveloped housing in existing urban areas near places of work. It calls for investing in public transit and improving urban services to make cities livable and affordable. But, as pointed out above, county officials have, for nearly six years, repeatedly resisted a court injunction to plan legally and soundly.

This history is reason, by itself, to have little faith in the county's ability to act as steward of our land. And there should be great concern over the county's recently unveiled 2020 plan.

If the county refuses the court's order to properly plan for the 200,000 acres of watershed, how can we trust it with nearly 500,000 acres of rural lands? In fact, the planning department is currently processing hundreds of projects that will convert thousands of acres of farm and rural lands into subdivisions and strip malls in East County, even while it talks smart growth. Incredibly, the county is attempting to sell a 2020 plan that spreads sprawl onto our rural lands and even tries to undo an overwhelmingly popular initiative protecting the Cleveland National Forest.

Is there any wonder that the county's clean water conference was boycotted by all the major environmental groups in San Diego? The conference met to discuss ways to protect San Diego's streams, rivers, reservoirs and the watersheds that feed them. At stake are not only the reservoirs that store imported water but also a large portion of the locally produced water San Diegans drink. But the Sierra Club, the Environmental Health Coalition, Audubon Society, Surfrider and Baykeeper reasonably thought that a government that has been acting to destroy these same watersheds and our beaches has no credibility at all.

We need to wake up from this bad dream and realize that we have no county elected officials with the integrity to resist the building industries' pressures. We can't count on them to do the right thing just because they sprinkle themselves with a little smart-growth pixie dust.

What we can do is:

  • Insist that our elected officials stop stalling on complying with state and federal laws. The county should not be appealing -- for the nth time -- the Superior Court's ruling on its illegal general plan and rezoning.
  • Demand that the Board of Supervisors enforce an urban limit line immediately. There is no such line in General Plan 2020. In fact, the plan eliminates regional planning categories, and thus, existing distinctions between urban, rural and wild areas.
  • Tell the supervisors to stop the election year posturing and get moving on an environmentally sound and legal general plan. If the county planning department is not up to the task, its top staff should resign.
  • Demand that the county declare a moratorium on large-scale subdivisions and commercial projects -- supermarket malls -- at the urban-rural fringe. Only real estate speculators want these projects.
  • Tell the county to stop calling handholding conferences and get to work with other public officials and build a community that benefits all its citizens. We've known what to do for 20 years.

Let's stop talking smart growth and do it.
