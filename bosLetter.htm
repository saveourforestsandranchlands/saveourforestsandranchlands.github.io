Re: GP 2020

Dear Board of Supervisors,

Save Our Forest And Ranchlands (SOFAR) is a grass-roots organization dedicated to the protection of the wilderness, watershed, agricultural, and scenic resources of San Diego County. We are comprised of concerned citizens and experts in the fields of land use, conservation of natural resources, land planning, and biology. In the past, we have submitted detailed comments on the GP 2020 planning process, and, again, reiterate our profound concern regarding the foundation of GP 2020 and the direction in which it is headed.

The County's so-called GP 2020 is based on the idea of accepting its share of a projected region-wide population increase. The County calls this density-based planning. This is a theoretical misstep. We have said before that a proper General Plan should be based on the sustainable use of finite resources, including both built and natural resources. These resources constitute the foundation of our regional community. The sustainable use of these resources is vital to the long term health and welfare of the region. Heretofore, such resources have been privatized and exploited in a non-sustainable way for the profit of a few. This must change. Planning for these foundational resources must be for the common good of all citizens.

The San Diego region is experiencing five structural crises in housing, transportation, beach pollution, infrastructure, and the environment. All these crises have the same root cause: inefficient, wasteful land-use planning. The question is not how to equitably distribute more people throughout a system that is dysfunctional and structurally failing, but rather how to best refurbish and redesign our built resources and protect our natural ones. Cities of comparable size with better urban design and transportation facilities have proven themselves capable of accommodating much higher densities and maintaining their quality of life.

Planners and the public agree (see Public Policy Institute survey) that the answer is an urban growth boundary. This line would clearly demarcate the built from the natural resources. Inside the boundary would be our urban areas. These areas are where our tax dollars should be redirected to encourage housing, improve traffic, clean up beach pollution, rebuild infrastructure, and protect the environment. Outside the boundary would be our farmland, open space, and wilderness areas. These are to be preserved.

The cities should be the areas to incorporate the bulk of the projected increase in population, not the outlying unincorporated County. The County should not be in the development business. It should act as guardians of the farmlands, rangelands, and open space of the backcountry as well as provide stewardship over our Country Towns. Only through implementing an urban growth boundary can we reinvest in our cities to make them attractive and vibrant places to live and work. At the same time, we can protect and preserve our agricultural and open space lands. First, protect the resources, then distribute the population, and separate one from the other with an urban growth boundary.


Sincerely,


Duncan McFetridge
President
