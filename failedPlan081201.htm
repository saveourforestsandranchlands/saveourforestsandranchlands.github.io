Supervisors ignore the public good

Union-Tribune Editorial

Failed planning
Supervisors ignore the public good

August 12, 2001

When a city such as San Diego, Chula Vista or Encinitas considers planning and zoning for a community, it doesn't just ask landowners and builders what they want. Cities consider what's best for the entire community, including impacts on traffic, the environment, quality of life and character of neighborhoods.

Otherwise, we might have convenience stores in the middle of residential blocks, industrial warehouses next to condominiums or office buildings on neighborhood streets.

While most elected city leaders and city planning departments understand that the wishes of property owners can't take precedence over the good of the community, the county Board of Supervisors and the county Department of Planning and Land Use have never understood it.

Over and over since the 1970s, the county has undertaken major planning efforts for backcountry land. Over and over, courts have struck down these plans because they didn't take into account the impacts on traffic, the environment, air quality, water resources, etc. Instead, county supervisors and county planners accommodated the wishes of landowners while virtually ignoring how backcountry residential and agricultural development would affect the rest of the county and the invaluable natural resources.

The county wasted millions of taxpayer dollars developing and unsuccessfully defending these deficient backcountry plans against lawsuits by Save Our Forests and Ranchlands (SOFAR). About a month ago, the county dropped its appeal of the latest court ruling against the latest county plan, this after county supervisors and planners swore they had finally gotten it right. The county has been sent back to the drawing board yet again; the judge ordered officials to rescind their latest backcountry plan.

After the county's losing streak began a decade ago, a judge turned over backcountry land-use authority to SOFAR because the county was failing its public mandate to plan and zone private land. Incredibly, this small, nonprofit conservation group now controls all decisions on building permits and other matters for 250,000 acres of privately owned agricultural and natural land. This may be unprecedented in the nation, and should be humiliating to county supervisors and planners.

After this latest defeat, the people of San Diego County should demand that the Board of Supervisors finally produce an honest plan for the backcountry, one that doesn't fundamentally alter its current rural and natural character and that forthrightly examines the real environmental impacts of land-use changes. Unless supervisors want SOFAR making backcountry decisions forever, they must recognize that their policy of indulging the wishes of backcountry landowners and builders, at the expense of the rest of the county and the environment, is both a legal failure and a land-use failure.
