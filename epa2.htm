Dear Supervisor Jacob:

On March 31, 2000 the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) sent you a letter regarding a proposed General Plan Amendment (and Environmental Impact Report) for rezoning agricultural lands in San Diego County (GPA 96-03). In that letter, EPA expressed concern with the proposed rezoning and potential negative environmental effects from significant land use changes. After discussing our letter with the County Planning staff this week, we acknowledge that EPA misinterpreted portions of the EIR and proposed amendment. The purpose of this letter is to clarify our comments and confirm our conversations with your staff. We hope that you will consider these revised comments at your Board hearing on April 5th.

EPA recognizes that the County’s intent with GPA 96-03 is to preserve agriculture, not to facilitate residential/commercial development. We understand that the amendment is a downzone (e.g. a decrease in allowable dwelling unit/acre density), not an upzone (as asserted in our 3/31/00 letter) since currently Ag20 lands are generally zoned at 1 dwelling unit/8 acres. Our concerns about the effects of GPA 96-03 on water quality degradation and habitat destruction have been somewhat alleviated, especially in light on County staff’s pointing out to us that of the 200,000 acres in question, about 50% are unsuitable for agriculture (or development) due to topography and water constraints, and about 25% of the lands are in public ownership. Furthermore, according to County staff, the recommendation calls for only about 18,000 acres to be at the 1:10 unit to acre ratio, while the remaining acreage would be at 1:40. However, we do want to note that this proposed amendment would still allow an increase in density above existing conditions.

We remain concerned with the County’s own conclusion that GPA 96-03 is expected to result in significant adverse impacts to the environment, including water and biological resources. It is this concern which prompted EPA’s letter and continues to hold our interest. GPA 96-03 could lead to low density development, even though further development is not contemplated at this time. While we understand that the General Plan Amendment seeks to preserve agriculture, we are seeing a rapid conversion of agricultural lands to residential and commercial uses in California, creating a significant environmental problem. Where we see conversion of agricultural land, we see loss and/or fragmentation of terrestrial and aquatic habitats, decrease in the region’s biodiversity, and degradation of water quality from increased pollutant loadings. We hope that this will not be the case in Ag20 lands.

We appreciate the opportunity to offer comments on this proposed General Plan Amendment. If there are any questions regarding our comments please contact Paul Michel at (415) 744-1999.

Sincerely,

Nancy Woo, Chief
