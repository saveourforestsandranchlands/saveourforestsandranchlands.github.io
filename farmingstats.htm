The Farm Bureau’s relationship to Farmland and Development in San Diego County

In July the California Farm Bureau entered an Amicus Brief in support of the County of San Diego and in opposition to the position held by SOFAR.

We, SOFAR, as defenders of the backcountry and the plaintiffs in this case, have to ask of the Farm Bureau, the so-called guardians of farms and the agricultural industry, Why do you get involved now?

Consider the devastation to agriculture in the County while you have remained motionless:

  • Since 1984, San Diego County has endured the loss of 30,000 acres of its farmland.

  • We’ve been ranked in the top 10 state counties suffering the loss of significant acreages of its irrigated agricultural lands for 8 years.

  • Farms of every size have been decreasing in numbers of acreages, even though the value of the crops produced skyrocketed.*

  • Development pressures adjacent to farms have pushed farmers out of business, threatening the very viability of remaining farms in rural areas with land speculation and conversion of use.

*due to the proliferation of ornamental crops which bring a higher dollar-value but which feed none.

General Plan Amendment 96-03 as currently put forward, and allegedly for the expansion and benefit of small farming in the County, is a total failure.

  • The County supposes that farming that is currently unsuccessful in areas with irrigation and service will be successful further out into the backcountry.

  • In GPA 96-03, the County proposes to eliminate agriculture as a required Element in the General Plan thereby leaving 300,000 acres of cropland outside agricultural preserves with no substantial protection. Today thousands of acres of productive cropland are in the planning stages, slated for development. Have you nothing to say about these?

  • Without an Agricultural Element to protect agriculture west of the County Water Authority, there is no guarantee that the conversion of grazing lands will turn into small farm agriculture as the County claims because they’re encouraging conversion of ag lands in areas with irrigation and services.
  • Assumption of total clearing over the land doesn’t benefit farming interests but it does lay the foundation for future development of ranchettes.

  • The lack of environmental review for farming and clearing the land is another method of the County shirking its duty to direct the use of the land in responsible manner. The County shifts the responsibility for environmental safeguards upon the farmer as a result of its own inability to plan effectively for the benefit of the region.

If, as your brief suggests, you think that the plan for San Diego’s agricultural lands will be a model for other counties in the state, SOFAR is concerned about where your true loyalties lie, for the deterioration in quality and number of agricultural lands and their diversity does not bode well for the sustainability of the industry.

Witness San Diego County’s dismal record over the last 20 years and it becomes evident that the real problem lies in the failure of leadership to ensure the long term viability and productivity of farming in San Diego. Could this be what the Farm Bureau envisions for the state?
